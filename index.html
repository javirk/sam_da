Abstract

This paper addresses the domain adaptation challenge for semantic segmentation in medical imaging. Despite the impressive performance of recent foundational segmentation models like SAM on natural images, they struggle with medical domain images. Beyond this, recent approaches that perform end-to-end fine-tuning of models are simply not computationally tractable. To address this, we propose a novel SAM adapter approach that minimizes the number of trainable parameters while achieving comparable performances to full fine-tuning. The proposed SAM adapter is strategically placed in the mask decoder, offering excellent and broad generalization capabilities and improved segmentation across both fully supervised and test-time domain adaptation tasks. Extensive validation on four datasets showcases the adapter's efficacy, outperforming existing methods while training less than 1% of SAM's total parameters.

Method

We aim to find the best combination of weak (class labels) and full annotations (segmentation maps). Starting from an initial point, defined by a small budget $B_0$ and a number of classificacion and segmentation samples ($C_0$ and $S_0$ respectively), at each iteration we sample the space and fit a Gaussian Process to the 3D points. Then, we use it to predict the Expected Improvement within the region up to the budget goal - $B$. Finally, we find a trajectory with ever-increasing Expected Improvement (EI) and select $(\Delta C, \Delta S)$ that improves EI by a fixed fraction of its maximum possible value.

Results

Main results

We compared our method in four datasets against different fixed strategies and an estimated best fixed strategy with $\alpha_s = 12$. We can make the following observations:

  • No single fixed strategy performs optimally across the different datasets evaluated.
  • The estimated best fixed strategy (in red) progressively loses competitiveness as budget increases.
  • Our method produces good performances consistently across different budget quantities and datsets.

Surfaces

We assume that segmentation performance grows logarithmically with data, and we impose that in the mean prior of the Gaussian Process: \[ \mu(C, S) = \gamma_c\log(\beta_cC+1) + \gamma_s\log(\beta_sS+1) \]

We see that this assumption holds in general for the four datasets but SUIM, hence the drop in performance for high budgets on this dataset.

Sensitivity to $\alpha_s$

Some datasets require different expertise and domain knowledge to annotate, and that is translated in different $\alpha_s$ in our model. We see that our method is robust regardless of the value of $\alpha_s$.
